Solar Energy

Solar energy is one of the most common sources of renewable energy. Solar panels are
made from subunits called solar cells. The most common solar cells are made of a
semiconductor called silicon. Silicon and crystals are sandwiched between conducting
layers. Each silicon atom is connected to its neighbors by 4 strong bonds, disallowing
movement of electrons. Solar cells have two different layers of silicon, one has extra
negatively charged electrons, and the second has extra space for electrons. Where the
two layers meet is called the PN junction. At the PN junction, electrons wander around
leaving a positive charge on one layer and a negative charge on the other. When a photon
with enough energy from the sunlight strikes the solar cell it can knock an electron from
its bond. The negatively charged electron and positive location of the hole move around.
The electron moves towards the N side and the hole moves towards the P side. The electric
field at the PN junction allows the electrons and the holes to move the opposite way from
each other. This creates a circuit and generates electricity since the electron drawn to
the side wants to go to the hole on the other side but can only do so by passing through
conducting wire. The movement of electrons creates electricity.
